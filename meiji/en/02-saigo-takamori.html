The Last Samurai Who Destroyed What He Built

Saig≈ç Takamori was the most powerful warrior of Japan's revolution ‚Äî the general who toppled the old regime, spared a city of a million people, and then raised an army against the very government he had helped create. His story is the tragedy at the heart of modern Japan.

In Tokyo's Ueno Park, one of the most visited public spaces in Japan, there stands a bronze statue of a stout man in a simple cotton robe, walking a dog. Tourists often pass it without a second glance, assuming it depicts some minor local dignitary or perhaps a beloved eccentric. The man is Saig≈ç Takamori. He was the supreme military commander of the revolution that ended feudal rule in Japan, the architect of one of the nineteenth century's most remarkable acts of mercy, and, in the final chapter of his life, the leader of the largest armed rebellion against the government he had helped bring into existence. He was declared a traitor by the emperor he had fought to restore, and then, after his death, posthumously forgiven by that same emperor and enshrined as a national hero.

His life poses a question that has no comfortable answer: What do you do when the revolution you fought for becomes something you can no longer recognize?

But the domain's internal hierarchy was savage in its rigidity. The gulf between upper and lower samurai was not a glass ceiling; it was a granite wall. No amount of talent, courage, or ambition could carry a man born into the lower ranks into the upper echelons. You were what your father was, and his father before him.

The Saig≈ç household contained sixteen people living under one roof, surviving on a stipend that barely covered rice. They were, in the language of bureaucracy, respectable. In the language of daily life, they were poor.

Wang Yangming, a sixteenth-century Chinese thinker, taught that knowledge and action are inseparable ‚Äî that to truly understand justice, you must act justly, and that moral knowledge without moral action is no knowledge at all. At sixteen, Saig≈ç entered the local magistrate's office as a junior clerk, spending his days traveling through the countryside collecting taxes and inspecting rural conditions. The work was unglamorous, but it gave him something that would prove invaluable: a firsthand understanding of how ordinary people lived, and a visceral hatred for the corruption and incompetence of those who governed them. He later expressed this conviction in words that remain striking in their directness: When officials rot from within, the common people suffer the consequences.

The Mercy of Edo

Edo in 1868 was one of the largest cities on Earth. Its population exceeded one million ‚Äî larger than Paris, comparable to London. The Tokugawa had ruled from Edo Castle for over 250 years, and the city's infrastructure, economy, and population were intimately bound to the shogunate's existence. The Paradox

Saig≈ç Takamori's life is a study in contradiction ‚Äî or perhaps in a consistency so deep that it looks, from the outside, like contradiction.

He overthrew the old order, then mourned the values it had embodied. He built a modern state, then died fighting its army. He was the revolution's greatest champion and its most famous casualty.

But consider the possibility that there was no contradiction at all. What if Saig≈ç, from beginning to end, was driven by a single principle ‚Äî not loyalty to any government, old or new, but loyalty to an idea of justice? The Tokugawa shogunate was unjust, and he helped destroy it. When the Meiji government, in his eyes, became unjust in its own way ‚Äî abandoning the warriors who had fought for it, pursuing modernization at the cost of the values that had made Japan worth fighting for ‚Äî he stood against it with equal conviction.

His own words offer the clearest window into his thinking. Keiten Aijin ‚Äî Revere Heaven, Love Humanity. Not "revere the shogun" or "revere the emperor" or "revere the state." Heaven. A moral order higher than any political institution, answerable to no earthly authority.

Did he get it right? That is a question that admits no easy answer. The Meiji government he opposed went on to build one of the most successful modernizing states in history. The samurai class he championed was, by any practical measure, an anachronism. His rebellion, however noble in its motivations, was a military catastrophe that cost thousands of lives.
